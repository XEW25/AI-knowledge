## Raw capture

### Core thesis
This paper proposes a new 8-bit floating-point format, HiFloat8 / HiF8, for deep learning training and inference. It is motivated by a practical weakness of standard FP8 training: E4M3 and E5M2 each make a tradeoff between precision and dynamic range, so real systems often need both formats rather than one unified format.

HiF8 is designed as a single 8-bit format with tapered precision, variable exponent/mantissa allocation, and denormal-range extension. The paper argues that this gives a better precision/range balance and can support both forward and backward passes under one format more easily than conventional FP8 choices.

### Main technical idea
The key design idea is a variable-length `dot field` that determines how many bits are assigned to exponent and mantissa depending on the encoded region. Instead of using a fixed exponent/mantissa split, HiF8 dynamically shifts the allocation so that precision is higher near the center of the distribution and range is larger for large-magnitude values.

### What the paper claims
- Existing FP8 formats force an awkward precision/range tradeoff.
- HiF8 provides tapered precision and near-FP16 dynamic range.
- A single format can be used across more of the training pipeline.
- With suitable rounding and scaling, HiF8 often matches FP16 mixed-precision training in simulation.

### Important caveat
All results in the paper are simulation-based. The paper is best interpreted as a format-design and feasibility study, not yet a definitive hardware-level validation.

### Why this paper matters
This is a useful starting source for the model-quantization line because it is not just about generic PTQ or low-bit inference. It is specifically about **training-friendly low-bit floating-point format design**, including dynamic range, precision allocation, and rounding strategy.
