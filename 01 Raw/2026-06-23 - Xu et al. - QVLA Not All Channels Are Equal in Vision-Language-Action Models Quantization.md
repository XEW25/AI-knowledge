### Abstract (verbatim)
The advent of Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models represents a significant leap for embodied intelligence, yet their immense computational demands critically hinder deployment on resource-constrained robotic platforms. Intuitively, low-bit quantization is a prevalent and preferred technique for large-scale model compression. However, we find that a systematic analysis of VLA model's quantization is fundamentally lacking. We argue that naively applying uniform-bit quantization from Large Language Models (LLMs) to robotics is flawed, as these methods prioritize passive data fidelity while ignoring how minor action deviations compound into catastrophic task failures. To bridge this gap, we introduce QVLA, the first action-centric quantization framework specifically designed for embodied control. In a sharp departure from the rigid, uniform-bit quantization of LLM-based methods, QVLA introduces a highly granular, channel-wise bit allocation strategy. Its core mechanism is to directly measure the final action-space sensitivity when quantizing each individual channel to various bit-widths. This process yields a precise, per-channel importance metric that guides a global optimization, which elegantly unifies quantization and pruning (0-bit) into a single, cohesive framework. Extensive evaluations on different baselines demonstrate the superiority of our approach. In the LIBERO, the quantization version of OpenVLA-OFT with our method requires only 29.2% of the original model's VRAM while maintaining 98.9% of its original performance and achieving a 1.49x speedup. This translates to a 22.6% performance improvement over the LLM-derived method SmoothQuant. Our work establishes a new, principled foundation for compressing VLA models in robotics, paving the way for deploying powerful, large-scale models on real-world hardware. Code will be released.

### Core thesis
First **action-centric** VLA quantization: instead of LLM-style uniform-bit / outlier-management (which optimizes feature/text fidelity), allocate bits **per output channel** by how much quantizing that channel perturbs the **final action output**. Unifies quantization + **pruning (0-bit)** in one framework.

### Method (verified, Sec. 3)
- **Sensitivity analysis (the motivation, Fig. 1)**: (a) per-module — vision encoder is robust; LLM is vulnerable; **projector + action head are most sensitive** (the multimodal→action interface). (b) per-channel — strong intra-layer heterogeneity ("not all channels are equal").
- **Action-space sensitivity metric**: quantize one channel `(l,c)` to bit `b∈{0,2,4,8,16}`, keep all else FP, measure action deviation. Single-step `s^(b)_{l,c}` = E‖Ã−A*‖² (Action-MSE, teacher-forcing); cumulative `S^(b)_{l,c}` over an episode (captures long-horizon error accumulation). Rankings of `s` and `S` are consistent → use cheap `s` to allocate, `S` to validate.
- **Fast first-order proxy**: Taylor/Jacobian — `‖ΔA‖ ≈ ‖J_{A,X_{l,c}}‖·‖ΔX_{l,c}‖`, Jacobian gain × quant noise → global ranking; then a few full forward passes to precisely calibrate the top channels. (Matches repo `sensitivity_hessian_proxy.py`.)
- **Optimal bit allocation (greedy demotion, Alg. 1)**: minimize Σ sensitivity s.t. average-bit budget B̄. Init all channels to 16-bit; demote in stages 16→8→4→2→0 by lowest **sensitivity-to-bit ratio** `ρ = (s_lo − s_hi)/(b_hi − b_lo)` (marginal error per saved bit) until budget met. 2→0 (pruning) regularized with dual-threshold + L0. Complexity O(C log C).
- **Granularity asymmetry (deliberate, hardware-friendly)**: **weights** = per-output-channel mixed bit-width {0,2,4,8,16}; **activations** = UNIFORM bit-width (e.g. 8-bit), distribution-aware calibration → **branch-free, stable latency** (avoids per-channel runtime branching / kernel fragmentation). Weights stored per-row with own scale/zp, dequantized on access.
- **What's kept FP16**: projector + action head (most sensitive) stay full BF16 to preserve control stability; gated allocation applies to vision backbone + language module.

### Models & results (verified)
- Base: **OpenVLA + OpenVLA-OFT** (autoregressive, Llama-2 7B). Generalization (appendix): **UniVLA-7B**, **CALVIN** benchmark. Real-world: **π0** (flow-matching) under W8A16. Hardware: RTX 4090 (sim) / 4070 (real-world deploy).
- **W4A4 (Tab. 1):** OpenVLA-OFT QVLA **96.0%** (FP 97.1, −1.1% / 98.9% retained), 4.5 GB (**29.2%**), **1.49× speedup**; vs SmoothQuant 73.4% (−23.7%), OmniQuant 93.9%. OpenVLA QVLA 76.0% (FP 76.5, 99.3% retained), 4.3 GB (28.2%), 1.47×.
- **Weight-only W4A16 (Tab. 2):** OpenVLA QVLA 76.5% (0.0% loss) vs AWQ −4.7%; OpenVLA-OFT QVLA −0.4% vs AWQ −4.5%.
- **Ablations**: channel-wise > layer-wise (INT4: 76.5 vs 74.8); prune(0-bit) helps (Tab. 4: {0,2,4,8,16} 76.8 vs uniform-8bit 74.6); temporal error accumulation (Fig. 3) — QVLA's 8-bit keeps lower cumulative error than uniform-8-bit, gap widening over the horizon.
- **Real-world (Tab. 5):** π0 W8A16, IMETA-Y1 dual-arm, 3 Orbbec cams; QVLA(AutoQVLA) ≈ FP (63.3% avg), **1.28× speedup**.
- **Speedup is reported as measured** (sim 1.47–1.49×, real 1.28×) — but the released eval is `fakew` (accuracy via simulated quant); the mixed-bit-weight real-kernel detail is the usual hard part (not fully specified beyond "branch-free uniform activations + per-row weight dequant").
- **Re-verified 2026-06-30 (arXiv full text):** the paper describes **no custom kernel** for the `{0,2,4,8,16}` mixed-bit weights — only *"Weights are stored on a per-row basis, each with its own scale and zero-point, and are dequantized upon access"* — gives **no timing methodology** (wall-clock vs estimate unstated), and names only the GPU (RTX 4090 sim; A100 train / 4070 deploy), no CUTLASS/backend. ⇒ The **1.49× is not reproducible from the released `fakew` artifact** (which measures accuracy only, in BF16). It is *bandwidth-plausible* — autoregressive OpenVLA decode is memory-bound, so a weight-only dequant kernel makes speedup ≈ weight-compression ratio, no INT-GEMM needed — but the figure itself is **unsubstantiated**. Sharp contrast: DyQ-VLA reports the same 1.49× on a **real CUTLASS kernel**, and even re-measures a QVLA-style static W4A4 at 1.49×/4.3 GB on its own backend (so the number is achievable on a real kernel — just not demonstrated by *QVLA's* artifact).
